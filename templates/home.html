My name is Cerek Hillen, and I've been programming since 2010 (when I was only 12). One of my friends, who was learning to program videogames, invited me to join him in his pursuit. He began to teach me C++. It didn't take long for me to surpass his ability. I learned the basics of program design, file IO, OOP, etc. After learning the basics, I tried my hand at installing Allegro to finally pursue making videogames. The difficulty of installing native bindings frustrated me. This anger towards C++ and the promise that Java was truly cross-platform fueled my switch in language.

I found, upon beginning to learn Java, that most of what I learned from C++ still applied. There were still classes and class hierarchies to design. There were still files to write to and read from. There were still game libraries to learn: like LWJGL. Thus I began to learn a very archeic form of OpenGL. Ancient functions were dug up: glBegin(), glVertex2f(), glVertex3f(), glEnd(), and others. I made basic games, such as Snake and Pong. Soon after I discovered Slick. With the streamlined API of Slick, I began to pursue creating more complex games. Despite it being a wonderful tool, I was not yet ready.

I had the concepts. I could make classes, I knew about pointers, recursion, basic algorithm design. I knew how to write each individual piece of code. But I didn't know how to manage it. I thought my skillset was just that of a programmer, not a team manager. But soon it began to creep on me that I would need to learn at least basic project management to make anything of note. To achieve this I switched between languages and projects, to expand my management ability in different contexts.

Around this time I also started to learn Haskell. A wonderful bit of the Haskell community is that they are so open to helping people with even the most abstract facets of programming. Not only that, but so much of the community is comprised of very skilled programmers (and computer scientists). I submitted projects for code reviews. They responded with far more than just Haskell advice. In a way, they taught me how to manage projects.

Through the Haskell community I developed as a person as well. I became someone who would much rather be uncomfortable and learn than stay comfortable and static. As this piece of my personality became more and more prevalent, I learned exponentially more about programming and computer science. I changed from a kid trying to program games to someone who can really understand, create, and manage reasonably sized projects.
